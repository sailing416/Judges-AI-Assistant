Protest Hearing
From receipt of the protest to the written decision
⚓
A protest is an allegation by a boat that another boat has broken a rule. The protest committee decides whether a rule was broken and, if so, what penalty applies. The authority for protests is RRS Part 5, principally rules 61–66.

The Six Stages of a Protest Hearing

  1. Receipt of Written Protest (Rule 61.2)A party delivers the protest in writing. Check that the protest was lodged within the time limit and that it names the boat protested.
  2. Validity (Rule 63.5)The protest committee determines whether the protest is valid before proceeding to a hearing. A protest is invalid if any requirement of rule 61.2 is not met, unless the protest committee decides to waive the requirement.
  3. Notification (Rule 63.2)All parties must be notified: time and place of the hearing, and the rule(s) alleged to have been broken.
  4. The Hearing (Rule 63)Conducted in accordance with rule 63. Parties present their cases, witnesses may be called, the committee may take evidence from others.
  5. Finding Facts and Applying Rules (Rule 64)The committee finds the facts and applies the relevant rules. If a rule is found to have been broken, the committee imposes a penalty or takes other action.
  6. Written Decision (Rule 65.1)The decision, with the facts found, the rules that apply, and the decision, shall be made available in writing to the parties upon request.

Step 1 — Validity of a Protest Rule 61.2 & 63.5

⚠️
The protest committee must determine validity before proceeding. If the protest is invalid, the hearing shall be closed. Rule 63.5

Requirements for a Valid Protest (Rule 61.2)

A protest by a boat shall be in writing and include:

  • the name of the protestor
  • the name of the boat protested
  • the incident, including where and when it occurred
  • the rule(s) believed to have been broken
  • the name and sail number, if known, of any witness the protestor intends to call

Time Limit Rule 61.3

A protest shall be delivered to the race office within the time limit set in the sailing instructions. If no time limit is set, the time limit is two hours after the last boat in the race finishes.

Hail Requirement Rule 61.1(a)

A boat intending to protest shall hail "Protest" as soon as reasonably possible after the incident. When the incident is not seen by the helmsman of the protesting boat, she need not hail but shall attempt to inform the protested boat at the first reasonable opportunity.

ℹ️
Waiver of Requirements: When the protest committee is satisfied that the requirements of a rule have been met to the extent possible given the circumstances, it may waive strict compliance with rule 61.2. Rule 63.5

If the Protest is Invalid

If the protest committee decides a protest is invalid, it shall so inform the parties and close the hearing. Rule 63.5. The written decision should record: (a) the fact that the protest was invalid, and (b) the reason(s) for invalidity.

Step 2 — Conducting the Hearing Rule 63

Right to be Present (Rule 63.2)

The parties to a protest are the protestor and the protestee. They, and any witnesses they call, are entitled to be present throughout the hearing. A party may have a representative present.

⚠️
A member of the protest committee who is involved in an incident being protested shall not take part in the hearing of that protest. Rule 63.4

Order of the Hearing (Rule 63.3)

  1. Read the protestThe chairman reads the protest aloud (or summarises the incident and rules alleged).
  2. Protestor presents caseThe protestor (or representative) describes the incident and may call witnesses.
  3. Protestee presents caseThe protestee (or representative) presents their version and may call witnesses. All parties may question each witness.
  4. Committee questions parties and witnessesThe committee may ask questions at any time. If new evidence arises, parties must have the opportunity to respond.
  5. Parties and witnesses leave the roomThe committee deliberates in private.
  6. Find facts and apply rulesThe committee agrees on the facts found and which rule(s) apply.
  7. Announce the decisionParties are recalled and the decision is announced. The written decision is to be made available upon request.

Evidence and Diagrams (Rule 63.6)

The committee may accept any evidence it deems necessary. Conflicting evidence should be carefully assessed. A diagram of the incident should be prepared — ideally by each party separately, then reconciled by the committee.

One Party Absent (Rule 63.3(b))

If a party to a hearing is not present, the committee may proceed with the hearing and the party is bound by the decision. If the protestee was not notified, the hearing shall be postponed.

Step 3 — The Decision Rules 64–65

Rule 64.1 — Penalties and Exoneration

When the protest committee decides that a boat that is a party to a protest has broken a rule and is not exonerated, it shall either disqualify her or impose another penalty if the rules so provide. Only one penalty shall be imposed on a boat for breaking one or more rules in an incident.

✅
Exoneration: A boat that breaks a rule of Part 2 while compelled to do so by another boat breaking a rule shall be exonerated. Rule 64.1(c)

Rule 65.1 — Informing Parties of the Decision

After making its decision, the protest committee shall promptly inform the parties of the facts found, the rules that apply, and the decision. If requested, it shall give them the written decision with the reasons.

ℹ️
Sail Canada Prescription (Rule 65.1): A boat that has been found by a protest committee to have broken a rule and caused damage shall be considered at fault for the purposes of rule 65.1.

What the Written Decision Must Contain

  • Event, date, race number
  • Names of protest committee members
  • Names of parties and their representatives
  • The facts found
  • The rules that apply
  • The decision (with reasons)
  • Signature of the chairman
  • Statement of the right of appeal (where applicable)

Decision-Making Criteria

🔍 Burden and Standard of Proof

There is no formal burden of proof on either party. The protest committee finds facts on the balance of probabilities — what is more likely to have happened. If the committee is unable to decide, the protest is dismissed. (Judges' Manual)

⚖️ Evaluating Conflicting Evidence

When parties give conflicting accounts, the committee should:

  1. Assess the consistency of each account with the physical facts.
  2. Consider whether each account is internally consistent.
  3. Assess the demeanour and credibility of witnesses.
  4. Consider independent evidence (video, GPS, third parties).
📐 Facts Found vs. Facts Alleged

The protest committee is not bound by the facts alleged in the protest. It decides what actually happened. It then applies the rules to those facts. The committee may find that a different rule than the one alleged was broken, and may penalise accordingly. Rule 64.1

🚩 Rule Application

Once facts are found, rule application is a matter of law. The committee should consult the Case Book for precedents. Cases decided by World Sailing are binding on all national authorities and their protest committees. RRS Introduction

Request for Redress
From receipt of the request to the written decision
⚖️
A request for redress is a request by a boat, or an action by the protest committee on its own initiative, to improve a boat's score or position in a race or series. The authority is Rule 62. Redress is not a protest — no rule need have been broken by the boat seeking redress.

The Five Stages of a Redress Hearing

  1. Receipt of Request or Own Initiative (Rule 62.2)A boat requests redress or the protest committee initiates the process on its own initiative. If initiated by a boat, the request must be in writing and within the time limit.
  2. Validity (Rule 63.5 applies by reference)The request must meet the requirements of rule 62. The protest committee must determine whether the request is valid before proceeding.
  3. Notification (Rule 63.2)All parties — the boat requesting redress and any other boats or persons who may be affected — must be notified of the time and place of the hearing.
  4. The Hearing (Rule 63)Conducted according to rule 63 in the same manner as a protest hearing, with parties presenting evidence.
  5. Decision (Rule 64.2)If the protest committee decides a boat is entitled to redress, it shall make as equitable an arrangement as possible for all boats affected.

Grounds for Redress Rule 62.1

A request for redress may only be made on one of the following grounds:

(a) Physical damage or injury from another boat that broke a rule

The boat suffered physical damage or a person was injured as a result of the action of a boat that broke a rule of Part 2, a rule of Part 4, or rule 42.

(b) Improper action or omission of the race committee

The race committee or protest committee made an improper action or omission that significantly worsened the boat's score or finishing position through no fault of the boat or those aboard her. Example: an incorrect recall signal, a course error, or a scoring error.

(c) Help to a person or vessel in danger

The boat gave help to a person or vessel in danger. This includes stopping to assist under rule 1.1.

(d) Injury or disability to a boat or person

A boat suffered injury or disability as the result of an act by a person not racing that was beyond the boat's control. Example: a spectator boat causing a wash at a critical moment.

⚠️
Redress is not available merely because a boat was disadvantaged. There must be a qualifying event under rule 62.1(a)–(d). The boat's score or finishing position must have been significantly worsened.

Conducting the Redress Hearing Rules 62–63

A redress hearing follows the same procedural rules as a protest hearing. Rules 63.2–63.6 apply. The key differences are:

Who are the parties?

The boat requesting redress is a party. If the request arises under rule 62.1(b) (improper action of the race committee), the race committee is a party. Any boats whose scores may be affected if redress is granted may be considered parties and should be notified.

Own-Initiative Hearings Rule 60.2(b)

The protest committee may initiate a redress hearing on its own initiative. This power should be used when the committee becomes aware of a scoring or procedural error that may have significantly affected a boat's result without that boat having requested redress.

Evidence Specific to Redress

The committee should gather evidence of: (1) the event giving rise to the request; (2) the boat's score or position but for the event; (3) the boat's actual score or position; (4) any effect of granting redress on other boats.

The Redress Decision Rule 64.2

Two-Stage Decision

The committee must first decide whether redress is deserved (i.e., whether the conditions of rule 62.1 are met), and then determine what redress is appropriate.

Stage 1 — Is the Boat Entitled to Redress?

The committee must find: (a) that one of the grounds in rule 62.1 applies; (b) that the boat's score or finishing position was significantly worsened; and (c) that this was through no fault of the boat or those aboard.

Stage 2 — What Redress is Appropriate?

Rule 64.2 requires the committee to make as equitable an arrangement as possible for all boats affected. Common forms of redress include:

  • Awarding a points score (e.g., average of the boat's other races)
  • Awarding the position the boat would have achieved
  • Abandoning the race for all boats
  • Adjusting time allowances
✅
Key Principle: The committee should aim for equity — the arrangement that most closely restores the boat to the position it would have been in, while not unfairly disadvantaging other boats.

Decision Criteria — Redress

🔍 Was the Score Significantly Worsened?

The worsening must be significant. A minor effect on the boat's score is insufficient. The committee should compare what score or position the boat would have achieved but for the qualifying event.

🚫 Fault of the Boat

Redress is not available if the boat or those aboard were at fault for the worsening. The test is whether those aboard acted reasonably in the circumstances.

⚖️ Equitable Arrangement

The committee has broad discretion in fashioning redress, but must consider the effect on all boats. Simply awarding first place to the requesting boat is seldom appropriate without thorough analysis of all boats' interests.

📖 Case Book Guidance

Consult the World Sailing Case Book for guidance on equitable arrangements. Cases such as WS Case 89 and WS Case 119 address common redress scenarios.

Appeals
Process from lodging an appeal to the written decision
📜
An appeal is a request to the national authority (Sail Canada in Canada) to review a decision of a protest committee on a question of the application of the rules. Appeals are governed by Rule 70 and Appendix R. In Canada, submit to: [email protected]
⚠️
Important: An appeal is not a new hearing of the facts. The appeal body does not re-hear witnesses. It reviews the protest committee's application of the rules to the facts found. Rule 71.3 & Appendix R

Who Can Appeal?

Under rule 70.1, a party to a protest or request for redress may appeal a decision of the protest committee on a question of the application of the rules, provided that there is no no-appeal protest committee or international jury in place for the event.

When Appeals Are Not Allowed Rule 70.4

  • When the sailing instructions state that the protest committee is a no-appeal protest committee
  • When an international jury has been appointed
  • When the protest committee's decision is on a question of fact (not rule application)
  • When the time limit for appeal has expired

Validity and Standing to Appeal Rules 70–71 & Appendix R

Who Has Standing?

Grounds for Appeal Rule 70.1

An appeal may only be made on a question of the application of the rules. The appeal body does not reconsider factual findings unless the facts found are self-contradictory or inadequate to support the decision reached.

Time Limit Appendix R, Rule R1.3

An appeal shall be lodged within 15 days of the receipt of the written decision. (Check applicable sailing instructions for event-specific time limits.)

Requirement to Provide Appendix R, Rule R2

The appellant shall provide: (a) a copy of the written protest or request; (b) the written decision; (c) a diagram of the incident if one was prepared; (d) a statement of the grounds of appeal.

ℹ️
Sail Canada Prescription (Rule 70.3(b)): Sail Canada so prescribes. This means that a party to a hearing may also request the national authority to review whether a protest committee followed the rules of Part 5 (procedural requirements).

The Appeal Process Appendix R

  1. Lodge the Appeal (Appendix R, Rule R1)The appellant submits the appeal in writing to Sail Canada at appeals@sailing.ca within 15 days of receipt of the written decision.
  2. Acknowledgment and Notification (Rule R3)The national authority acknowledges receipt and notifies the other parties that an appeal has been lodged.
  3. Protest Committee Response (Rule R3)The protest committee is invited to comment on the appeal. All parties may respond to each other's submissions.
  4. Review of the Record (Rule R4)The appeal body reviews the written record: the protest, the written decision, any diagrams, the facts found, and the parties' submissions. It does not hear new evidence on the facts.
  5. Decision (Rule 71)The appeal body may uphold the protest committee's decision, change it, or refer it back with instructions. It shall give its reasons in writing.
  6. The Decision is Final (Rule 71.4)The decision of the national authority on an appeal is final and shall not be appealed further (subject to the Sail Canada prescription allowing appeal to Sail Canada from a provincial appeals committee).
ℹ️
Sail Canada Prescription (Appendix R): Sail Canada may delegate the responsibilities in rules 71.2–71.5, R3, R4 and R5 to a provincial appeals committee. Decisions of a provincial appeals committee may be further appealed to Sail Canada.

The Appeal Decision Rule 71

What the Appeal Body May Do Rule 71.2

The national authority may:

  • Uphold the protest committee's decision
  • Change the decision (including a change to the penalty)
  • Refer the protest back to the protest committee with instructions to hold a new hearing or to decide specific questions

Finality Rule 71.4

The decision of the national authority on an appeal is final and may not be appealed further (except where Sail Canada's prescription allows further appeal from a provincial appeals committee to Sail Canada).

Written Decision Requirements

The written decision of the appeal body shall include: (a) the facts as found by the protest committee; (b) the question(s) on appeal; (c) the analysis and reasons; (d) the decision.

Decision Criteria — Appeals

🔍 Scope of Review

The appeal body does not disturb findings of fact unless they are self-contradictory, or the facts found are inadequate to support the legal conclusion reached. The appeal body reviews only the application of the rules to the facts found.

📐 Adequacy of Facts Found

If the facts found by the protest committee are insufficient to allow the appeal body to decide the question of rule application, the appeal body may refer the matter back to the protest committee to find additional facts. It does not find its own facts.

📖 World Sailing Cases

The appeal body shall have regard to the World Sailing Case Book. Cases decided by World Sailing are binding on all national authorities and their protest committees. RRS Introduction

⚖️ Procedural Fairness

If a party was not given the opportunity to be present or to present its case, the appeal body may set aside the protest committee's decision on grounds of procedural unfairness and refer the matter back for a new hearing.

Appeals vs. Redress — Which Process?
Guidance on when each procedure is appropriate
🧭
This guide is to help you determine which procedure is appropriate in a given situation. Using the wrong procedure will result in the matter being dismissed or referred to the correct process.
Factor Protest Rule 61 Redress Rule 62 Appeal Rule 70
What is being challenged? An alleged breach of a racing rule by another boat in a race A significant worsening of a boat's score due to one of the rule 62.1 grounds A protest committee's application of the rules to the facts it found
Who initiates? A boat that was a party to the incident, or the race/protest committee A boat seeking redress, or the protest committee on its own initiative A party to the original hearing
Is a rule breach required? Yes — a rule must be alleged Not necessarily — the boat's own score is affected through no fault of its own N/A — the question is whether the committee applied the rules correctly
When? Within the protest time limit after the race Within the time limit after knowing the grounds (rule 62.2) Within 15 days of receiving the written decision
Who hears it? Protest committee Protest committee National authority (Sail Canada) or provincial appeals committee
Facts re-heard? Yes — full hearing of evidence Yes — full hearing of evidence No — only the written record is reviewed
Result Penalty, exoneration, or dismissal Equitable arrangement or dismissal Uphold, change decision, or refer back

Common Scenarios and the Correct Process

Scenario 1 — Boat A believes Boat B broke rule 11 (windward/leeward)

→ Protest under rule 61. Boat A must hail "Protest", display a flag if required, and deliver a written protest within the time limit.

Scenario 2 — Boat C was recalled incorrectly by the race committee

→ Request for Redress under rule 62.1(b). The race committee made an improper action that significantly worsened Boat C's score through no fault of Boat C.

Scenario 3 — Boat D stopped to assist a capsized sailor and finished last

→ Request for Redress under rule 62.1(c). Boat D gave help to a person in danger under rule 1.1.

Scenario 4 — Boat E was penalised but believes the committee misapplied rule 15

→ Appeal under rule 70. Boat E is a party to the protest hearing, and the question is whether the committee correctly applied rule 15 to the facts it found.

Scenario 5 — Boat F believes the protest committee made an error of procedure

→ Appeal under rule 70.3(b) (Sail Canada prescription). A party may request the national authority to review whether the protest committee followed the requirements of Part 5.

Scenario 6 — Boat G was damaged by a spectator powerboat

→ Request for Redress under rule 62.1(d). Boat G suffered an act by a person not racing that was beyond its control.

Example — Protest Upheld (Rule 11)

WRITTEN DECISION OF THE PROTEST COMMITTEE Event: Laser Fleet Championship 2025 Race: Race 3 Date: 15 July 2025 Hearing: Protest Protest Committee: A. Judge (Chairman), B. Brown, C. Smith Protestor: GBR 123 (Blue Boat), John Sailor Protestee: GBR 456 (Red Boat), Jane Helm FACTS FOUND On the run to the leeward mark, GBR 123 (leeward boat) and GBR 456 (windward boat) were sailing on port tack in close proximity. GBR 456 was to windward of GBR 123 and was overlapping. GBR 456 was not keeping clear. GBR 456 made contact with GBR 123, resulting in damage to GBR 123's spinnaker pole. GBR 456 did not take a penalty at the time of the incident. RULES THAT APPLY Rule 11 (On the Same Tack, Overlapped): When boats are on the same tack and overlapped, a windward boat shall keep clear of a leeward boat. Rule 14 (Avoiding Contact): A boat shall avoid contact with another boat if reasonably possible. DECISION GBR 456 broke rule 11 by failing to keep clear of GBR 123 as required when boats are on the same tack and overlapped. GBR 456 also broke rule 14 by failing to avoid contact when it was reasonably possible to do so. GBR 456 is DISQUALIFIED from Race 3. Parties are reminded that they have a right of appeal to Sail Canada (appeals@sailing.ca) within 15 days of receipt of this written decision, provided that no no-appeal protest committee or international jury was in place. Signed: A. Judge (Chairman) Date: 15 July 2025

WRITTEN DECISION OF THE PROTEST COMMITTEE Event: Regional Offshore Series 2025 Race: Race 2 Date: 22 August 2025 Hearing: Request for Redress Protest Committee: A. Judge (Chairman), B. Brown, C. Smith Requesting Boat: CAN 99 (Windy), Peter Captain Other Party: Race Committee FACTS FOUND CAN 99 was one of 12 boats racing. The race committee displayed an individual recall signal for CAN 99 at the start. Upon review of the race committee's video evidence and the GPS track of CAN 99, the protest committee finds that CAN 99 was not over the line at the start. The recall was incorrectly issued. CAN 99, having been signalled to return, re-started and finished in 9th place. Had she not been recalled, she would have finished in 2nd place. RULES THAT APPLY Rule 62.1(b): A boat may request redress if her score has been significantly worsened through an improper action or omission of the race committee. Rule 64.2: When the protest committee decides that a boat is entitled to redress, it shall make as equitable an arrangement as possible for all boats affected, which may be to adjust the boat's score. DECISION The protest committee finds that: (a) The race committee made an improper action by incorrectly recalling CAN 99; (b) CAN 99's finishing position was significantly worsened through no fault of CAN 99; (c) Redress is deserved. CAN 99 is awarded 2nd place in Race 2. All other boats' finishing positions remain unchanged. Parties are reminded of their right of appeal to Sail Canada (appeals@sailing.ca) within 15 days. Signed: A. Judge (Chairman) Date: 22 August 2025

13While TackingA boat tacking shall keep clear of boats not tacking until she is on a close-hauled course.

15Acquiring Right of WayWhen a boat acquires right of way, she shall initially give the other boat room to keep clear.
16Changing CourseWhen a right-of-way boat changes course, she shall give the keep-clear boat room to keep clear.
17On Same Tack; Proper CourseA boat that becomes overlapped to leeward within two of her hull lengths shall not sail above her proper course.
18Mark-RoomWhen boats are about to round or pass a mark, the outside boat shall give the inside boat mark-room.
19Room to Pass an ObstructionA right-of-way boat shall give room to pass an obstruction to a boat that is entitled to room.
20Room to Tack at an ObstructionA boat may hail for room to tack at an obstruction when she is sailing close-hauled or above.
21ExonerationA boat shall be exonerated if she breaks a rule of Part 2 while taking room she is entitled to.
